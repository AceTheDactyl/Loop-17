The Fifth Postulate: Euclid's Parallel Lines

The Most Famous Postulate in Mathematics

Euclid's fifth postulate—the parallel postulate—stands apart from his first four axioms. While the first four are simple and obviously true, the fifth is complex and non-obvious:

Euclid's Fifth Postulate (Parallel Postulate)
"If a straight line falling on two straight lines makes the interior angles on the same side less than two right angles, the two straight lines, if produced indefinitely, meet on that side on which the angles are less than two right angles."

Or, in Playfair's simpler equivalent formulation:

Through any point not on a given line, there is exactly one line parallel to the given line.

2000 Years of Doubt

For over two millennia, mathematicians tried to prove the fifth postulate from the first four. Its complexity seemed suspicious—surely it could be derived? But every attempt failed.

The fifth postulate is actually independent—neither provable nor disprovable from the other axioms. This realization in the 19th century led to the revolutionary discovery of non-Euclidean geometries:
