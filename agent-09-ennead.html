The Nine-Point Circle

In Euclidean geometry, the nine-point circle (Euler's circle, Feuerbach's circle) is a circle that passes through nine significant points of any triangle. This elegant result unifies multiple triangle centers into a single circular locus.

The Nine Points

Three midpoints: Midpoints of the three sides of the triangle

Three feet: Feet of the three altitudes

Three Euler points: Midpoints of the segments from vertices to the orthocenter

R₉ = R/2

The radius of the nine-point circle is exactly half the circumradius

Feuerbach's Theorem

The nine-point circle is tangent to the incircle and the three excircles of the triangle. The point of tangency with the incircle is called the Feuerbach point.

d(N, I) = |R/2 - r|

where N = nine-point center, I = incenter, R = circumradius, r = inradius

Casting Out Nines: Base-10 Repdigit Test

One of the most elegant properties of 9 in base-10 arithmetic is the digital root and casting out nines technique, which provides a quick divisibility check and error detection method.

Digital Root Theorem

n ≡ Σ(digits of n) (mod 9)
Digital Root(n) = 1 + ((n-1) mod 9)

Any number is congruent to the sum of its digits modulo 9.
Example: 12345 → 1+2+3+4+5 = 15 → 1+5 = 6
12345 mod 9 = 6 ✓

Divisibility by 9

A number is divisible by 9 if and only if the sum of its digits is divisible by 9.

Example: 7326
7+3+2+6 = 18
1+8 = 9
∴ 7326 ≡ 0 (mod 9)
7326 = 9 × 814 ✓

Arithmetic Verification

Casting out nines can verify arithmetic operations:

123 × 456 = 56088
DR(123) = 6
DR(456) = 6
DR(6×6) = DR(36) = 9
DR(56088) = 9 ✓

Why 9 Works This Way

In base 10, 10 ≡ 1 (mod 9), therefore:
10ⁿ ≡ 1ⁿ ≡ 1 (mod 9) for all n

So a number written as: aₙ×10ⁿ + aₙ₋₁×10ⁿ⁻¹ + ... + a₁×10 + a₀
reduces to: aₙ + aₙ₋₁ + ... + a₁ + a₀ (mod 9)

This is because 10 = 9 + 1, making 9 the repdigit modulus for base-10.
