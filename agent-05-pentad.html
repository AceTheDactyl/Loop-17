Penrose Tiling and Quasicrystals

The Impossible Made Real

For centuries, mathematicians believed five-fold symmetry was impossible in periodic tilings. Regular pentagons cannot tile the plane—there will always be gaps or overlaps. This seemed to close the door on pentagonal order in crystals.

Then in 1974, Roger Penrose discovered something extraordinary: aperiodic tilings with perfect five-fold symmetry. Using just two shapes (kites and darts, or thick and thin rhombi), both with angles related to φ, Penrose created tilings that never repeat but exhibit long-range pentagonal order.

Penrose Rhombi Angles
Thin rhombus: 36° and 144° (π/5 and 4π/5)
Thick rhombus: 72° and 108° (2π/5 and 3π/5)
All angles are multiples of 36° = π/5
Ratio of tile frequencies = φ (golden ratio)

Quasicrystals: Five-Fold Symmetry in Matter

In 1982, Dan Shechtman discovered something thought impossible: a crystal structure with five-fold symmetry. His electron diffraction pattern of an aluminum-manganese alloy showed perfect pentagonal symmetry—a "quasicrystal" with Penrose-like atomic arrangement.

This discovery, initially met with skepticism, revolutionized crystallography and earned Shechtman the 2011 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. Nature had found a way to create five-fold order through aperiodic arrangement.

Quasicrystal Properties

  • Aperiodic: Never repeating, but not random
  • Five-fold symmetry: Impossible in periodic crystals
  • Golden ratio: Frequency ratios of local patterns follow φ
  • Long-range order: Despite aperiodicity, show structural order
  • Natural occurrence: Found in meteorites and geological formations
  • Applications: Ultra-hard coatings, LED lights, frying pans
"There are no such things as quasicrystals, only quasi-scientists." — Linus Pauling (before being proven wrong)

The Fifth Postulate: Euclid's Parallel Lines

The Most Famous Postulate in Mathematics

Euclid's fifth postulate—the parallel postulate—stands apart from his first four axioms. While the first four are simple and obviously true, the fifth is complex and non-obvious:

Euclid's Fifth Postulate (Parallel Postulate)
"If a straight line falling on two straight lines makes the interior angles on the same side less than two right angles, the two straight lines, if produced indefinitely, meet on that side on which the angles are less than two right angles."

Or, in Playfair's simpler equivalent formulation:

Through any point not on a given line, there is exactly one line parallel to the given line.

2000 Years of Doubt

For over two millennia, mathematicians tried to prove the fifth postulate from the first four. Its complexity seemed suspicious—surely it could be derived? But every attempt failed.

The fifth postulate is actually independent—neither provable nor disprovable from the other axioms. This realization in the 19th century led to the revolutionary discovery of non-Euclidean geometries:

Euclidean Geometry

Fifth postulate TRUE

Flat space

Parallel lines: Exactly 1

Triangle angles: = 180°

Curvature: 0

Hyperbolic Geometry

Fifth postulate FALSE

Negatively curved space

Parallel lines: Infinitely many

Triangle angles: < 180°

Curvature: negative

Elliptic Geometry

Fifth postulate FALSE

Positively curved space

Parallel lines: Zero

Triangle angles: > 180°

Curvature: positive

The fifth postulate thus represents a choice—a fork in the road of mathematical reality. Einstein would later discover that physical space itself is non-Euclidean, curved by mass and energy.

Why Five is Special

The fifth postulate stands alone among Euclid's axioms. The first four are about existence and uniqueness— constructing points, lines, and circles. But the fifth is about global structure—how space itself behaves at infinity.

Just as the number 5 introduces φ and breaks the rational order of 1-4, the fifth postulate introduces a degree of freedom that generates entire universes of geometry. It is the axiom that could not be proven, just as φ is the number that cannot be rationalized.

Five-Fold Symmetry: The Crystallographic Restriction

One of the deepest facts about 5 is the crystallographic restriction theorem: in two dimensions, periodic tilings (crystals) can only have rotational symmetries of order 1, 2, 3, 4, or 6. Five-fold symmetry is forbidden.

Why 5 is impossible in periodic crystals:
Pentagon interior angle: 108°
360° / 108° = 3.333... (not an integer)
For tiling: need 360° / angle = integer
Triangle (60°): 360/60 = 6 ✓
Square (90°): 360/90 = 4 ✓
Hexagon (120°): 360/120 = 3 ✓
Pentagon (108°): 360/108 = 3.333... ✗

This "impossibility" makes five-fold symmetry special. When it appears in nature (flowers, starfish, quasicrystals), it signals something beyond simple periodicity—a higher order of organization.
